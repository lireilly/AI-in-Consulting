Skills That Still Matter

What Firms Now Prioritize

As AI handles more data processing and pattern recognition, consulting firms have converged on six human capabilities that AI cannot replicate. These showed up in McKinsey's own competency frameworks after their AI integration.

✍️
Effective prompting & question formulation
Getting useful AI output starts with asking the right questions — which mirrors elite consulting communication.
🔍
Critical evaluation of AI outputs
AI can be confidently wrong. Spotting gaps, biases, and unsupported claims in AI responses is now core.
⚖️
Knowing when to trust vs. challenge AI
Not every AI recommendation is wrong — but knowing when to push back separates great analysts from good ones.
💡
Novel approaches & fresh perspectives
AI recombines existing patterns; it rarely generates genuinely new strategic framings. That's still a human edge.
🤝
Understanding client-specific requirements
AI doesn't know your client's culture, politics, or unspoken constraints. Context-gathering from humans is irreplaceable.
🚀
Translating AI insights into action
An AI surfaces patterns. A consultant turns those patterns into a strategy a client can actually execute.

Scenario A

Retail Client: AI Recommends Closing 40% of Stores

You are using AI to analyze a retail client's sales data. The AI recommends closing 40% of their store locations to cut costs and concentrate on high performers.

⚠️
This recommendation could affect thousands of employees and permanently reshape the client's brand. Before presenting it to the client, you must interrogate it.

Your task: Write 3–5 critical questions you would ask before acting on this AI recommendation. Think about: what data might be incomplete? What context does AI typically miss?

💡 Hint — what to consider

Think about: time window (is the data from one bad quarter?), seasonal variation, lease obligations, local community role of stores, brand perception vs. revenue, and whether the AI was trained on comparable retail categories. Ask: what would closing look like operationally?

Scenario B

Healthcare Market Entry: Write Three Levels of Prompts

You are helping a healthcare company evaluate entering a new market. You want to use AI to research market entry strategies — but the quality of output depends entirely on how you prompt it.

Your task: In the text area below, write three prompts — a basic, an intermediate, and an expert-level prompt. Label each clearly. Notice how specificity and structure change what you'd expect to get back.

💡 What makes an expert-level prompt?

Expert prompts include: a defined role for the AI (Act as a healthcare market analyst…), specific constraints (company size, geography, timeline), a structured output request (rank by risk-adjusted upside, flag failure modes), and explicit context the AI wouldn't otherwise assume.

Case Brief: Talbot Trucks

Talbot Trucks is a European commercial truck manufacturer evaluating whether to begin manufacturing electric trucks (eTrucks) to reduce its carbon footprint. They need to determine if this investment makes financial and strategic sense.

CustomersLarge trucking companies + small owner-operators
Current TechDiesel engines — fast refueling at existing gas stations
New TecheTrucks — e-motors, batteries, slower charging infrastructure build-out
Core ChallengeThis transition is disruptive for both the manufacturer AND their customers
Regulatory ContextEU carbon regulations are tightening; subsidies for EVs vary by market
Competitive ContextCompetitors (Volvo, Daimler) are already investing in eTruck platforms
🤔
Before answering anything: notice the tension here. Talbot needs to reduce carbon footprint for regulatory and reputational reasons — but their customers depend on operational efficiency (charging takes longer than refueling). Whose priorities drive the decision?

Talbot Trucks:
Write Your Prompts

🎯 After this module you will be able to formulate AI prompts at multiple levels of sophistication for a real consulting case study.
💭
Your hypothesis from the last module matters here. The best prompts are driven by specific hypotheses, not open-ended fishing. If you believe charging infrastructure is the key constraint, your prompt should target that directly.

Prompt Framework

Write three prompts: one broad (sets up the landscape), one targeted (investigates a specific hypothesis), and one that asks AI to challenge your thinking. Each builds on the last.

📖 See prompt structure guide

Broad prompt: Establish the landscape. Summarize the current state of electric commercial vehicle adoption in Europe, including regulatory drivers, key players, and infrastructure readiness as of 2025.

Targeted prompt: Test a hypothesis. Compare the total cost of ownership (TCO) for diesel vs. eTrucks for a small European owner-operator over 5 years, factoring in fuel/charging costs, maintenance, and battery replacement at 300,000 km.

Challenge prompt: Ask AI to steelman the opposite view. What are the strongest arguments against Talbot Trucks investing in eTruck manufacturing in 2025? Include financial, operational, and competitive risks.

Talbot Trucks:
Validate & Challenge the AI Output

🎯 After this module you will be able to critically evaluate an AI output — identifying what is reliable, what is missing, and what assumptions need to be challenged.
🧠
Imagine the AI gave you a response to your prompts from Module 5 — covering EV adoption stats, cost comparisons, and competitive dynamics. Before you use it in a client presentation, you must run it through three validation moves. Work through each one below.

The Three Validation Moves

Top consultants use these three questions systematically before acting on any AI output. Work through each one for the Talbot Trucks case.

Validate Reliability
Ask: "What information seems reliable? What needs external verification?" Think about data sources, recency (AI training cutoffs), and hallucination risk on specific numbers.
💡 What to look for
AI is reliable on well-documented, public, stable facts (major regulatory frameworks, general EV adoption trends). It is unreliable on: specific pricing for new technologies, recent policy changes, country-specific infrastructure data, and any statistic with a specific decimal. Always verify specific numbers against primary sources.

Identify Gaps
Ask: "What did the AI miss? What client-specific context isn't captured?" AI works from publicly available data — it has no knowledge of Talbot's internal costs, customer relationships, or fleet contract structures.
💡 Common gaps in AI consulting outputs
Typical AI gaps in a case like this:
  • Internal financial position (can Talbot afford CapEx?)
  • Existing fleet contracts and their terms
  • Union / labor implications of manufacturing shift
  • Talbot's specific customer geographic routes (charging gaps matter differently in rural Poland vs. the Ruhr Valley)
  • Competitive intelligence beyond public announcements
0 / 40 min
Challenge Assumptions
Ask: "What is the AI assuming that might not hold?" For example — an AI response might assume eTrucks have lower maintenance costs. But what about battery replacement at 300,000 km? Or charging infrastructure availability in remote Scandinavian routes?
💡 Examples of hidden AI assumptions in this case
  • Assumes charging infrastructure is broadly available — not true for long-haul routes through Eastern Europe or Scandinavia
  • Assumes consistent electricity pricing — industrial electricity rates vary significantly across EU member states
  • Assumes owner-operators have capital for eTruck upfront cost — small operators often finance trucks; eTruck financing is less mature
  • Assumes EU EV subsidies are stable — subsidy programs have changed significantly in Germany and France recently

Step 2 — Write Your Recommendation

Consulting Recommendation Structure

LeadState your recommendation directly in one sentence. (Yes/No/Conditional — don't hedge.)
RationaleGive your 2–3 strongest supporting reasons. Cite both AI-assisted findings and your own human analysis.
Key RiskName the single most important risk or condition that could change your recommendation.
Next StepWhat one action should Talbot take first?
📋 See an example structure (not a model answer)

Lead: Talbot should pursue a phased eTruck investment, beginning with large fleet customers in well-served corridors rather than full product line conversion.

Rationale: EU regulatory pressure makes inaction increasingly costly; large fleet operators have the capital and route predictability to absorb charging constraints; competitors' early investment creates first-mover learning advantages Talbot cannot afford to cede entirely.

Key Risk: EU subsidy stability — if Germany and France restructure EV industrial subsidies before 2027, the TCO case for large fleet operators weakens significantly.

Next Step: Commission a route-level infrastructure analysis for Talbot's top 10 fleet customers before any capital commitment.
