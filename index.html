Part 5 of 6 — Critical Evaluation

Evaluate AI Outputs & Build Your Recommendation

What you'll be able to doWrite an expert-level AI research prompt, critically evaluate AI-generated research for reliability, gaps, and hidden assumptions, then build a structured recommendation.

Step 1 — Write an Expert-Level AI Prompt

Before evaluating AI outputs, you need to know what to ask for. Using everything you've learned about Talbot's situation, write a single expert-level prompt you would use to gather decision-critical information for this case.

🎯
What makes a prompt "expert-level"? It assigns a clear role to the AI, specifies the company context, states the exact decision being made, structures the expected output (rank, compare, flag), and names the constraints — geography, timeline, customer segment — that matter most.
0 characters

Step 2 — Validate AI Outputs

A strong consultant doesn't just use AI output — they interrogate it. Answer all three questions below to practice the key validation moves: reliability checking, gap identification, and assumption challenging. All three are required to continue.

1. Reliability check: You asked AI to research eTruck adoption in Europe. Which type of information would you trust most from AI output without additional verification?
✓ Good choice. AI is most reliable for broad, well-documented trends — like the general direction of EU regulation or EV adoption trajectories. For specific figures (market share, exact subsidy amounts, quarterly earnings), always verify against primary sources before presenting to a client.
2. Gap identification: Which factors is AI least likely to capture accurately when researching Talbot's specific situation? (Select all that apply)
✓ Key insight. AI struggles most with company-specific, confidential, or relationship-based context: union agreements, supplier contracts, and specific customer fleet schedules. These are gathered through direct client interviews — and they're often the factors that make or break a recommendation.
3. Assumption challenge: Which is the most problematic hidden assumption an AI might make about Talbot's eTruck business case?
✓ Strong critical thinking. Treating all customers as one group is one of the most common — and most damaging — AI oversimplifications in B2B consulting. A large fleet operator with dedicated charging depots has fundamentally different eTruck economics than a small owner-operator on rural routes. Disaggregating by customer segment is often where the real insight lives.

Step 3 — Build Your Recommendation

Use the SCQ framework (Situation → Complication → Question → Answer) to structure your recommendation. The first three elements are pre-filled from the case.

Situation:Talbot Trucks is a European manufacturer considering eTruck production to reduce carbon footprint and respond to EU regulatory pressure. Complication:The transition requires significant CAPEX and disrupts both Talbot and its customers, who rely on diesel infrastructure today. Question:Should Talbot invest in eTruck manufacturing — and under what conditions?

🎯
Consulting insight: A strong recommendation is specific about conditions, honest about risks, and makes a clear call. The most common interview mistake is hedging every conclusion out of fear of being wrong. Be direct — and be ready to defend it.
